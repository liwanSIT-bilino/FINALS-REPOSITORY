Local Foods and Products of Mountain Province

1. Barlig

• Linapet (Local Food)

Linapet is a traditional rice delicacy that originates from the municipality of Barlig. It is made from ground sticky rice mixed with peanuts or sesame seeds, then wrapped in banana leaves before steaming. The dish is known for its mildly nutty flavor and firm but chewy texture, making it a staple during rituals and family gatherings. Locals prepare Linapet as an offering during harvest thanksgiving, symbolizing gratitude for a bountiful rice yield. Its preparation method has been passed down for generations, making it one of Barlig’s most authentic cultural foods.

• Barlig Heirloom Rice (Local Product)

Barlig is home to ancient terraces producing heirloom rice cultivated by the indigenous communities of the region. These rice varieties are organically grown using traditional farming techniques that do not involve chemicals or modern machinery. The grains are known for their deep aroma, rich flavor, and high nutritional value compared to commercial rice varieties. Farmers maintain centuries-old terrace systems, ensuring water sustainability and soil fertility. Barlig heirloom rice remains one of the most valued agricultural products of the municipality and is often sold as a premium product.

2. Bauko

• Patopat (Local Food)

Patopat is a sticky rice delicacy originally from Bauko, and it is known for being wrapped in intricately woven coconut or palm leaves. The preparation involves cooking glutinous rice mixed with sugar, giving it a caramel-like sweetness. Its leaf container not only protects the rice but also enhances its aroma as it steams. Patopat is commonly served during community festivities, harvest celebrations, and family gatherings. This delicacy represents the creativity and agricultural heritage of Bauko.

• Bauko Arabica Coffee (Local Product)

Bauko is one of the leading producers of Arabica Coffee in Mountain Province, known for its smooth, mild acidity and floral aroma. Farmers cultivate coffee at high altitudes, which enhances bean quality and flavor profile. The coffee is grown under natural shade trees, making it both sustainable and environmentally friendly. Local farmers still use traditional hand-picking and sun-drying processes to maintain quality. Bauko Arabica is widely recognized in specialty markets and is one of the barangay’s strongest agricultural assets.

3. Besao

• Pinuneg (Local Food)

Pinuneg, or Igorot blood sausage, originates from Besao where it has been made for countless generations. It is prepared using fresh pig’s blood, minced meat, and mountain herbs, giving it a savory and earthy flavor. Pinuneg is traditionally served during rituals and feasts, especially after community butchering ceremonies. Its unique taste and cultural value make it one of Besao’s most distinctive foods. The dish symbolizes unity within the community as it is often shared among families during major celebrations.

• Besao Woven Baskets (Local Product)

Besao is known for its handcrafted woven baskets made from rattan and other forest materials. These baskets are traditionally used for carrying vegetables, rice, and tools across mountainous terrain. Artisans carefully weave each piece using ancestral techniques that produce durable and flexible designs. The baskets come in different sizes depending on their purpose, from household use to agricultural work. Besao’s weaving tradition is an important cultural livelihood and remains a symbol of resilience and craftsmanship.

4. Bontoc

• Inanchila (Local Food)

Inanchila originates from Bontoc and is one of its most well-known delicacies. It is a sticky rice dessert prepared using pounded rice shaped into small pieces and cooked in coconut milk. The texture is soft and chewy, while the flavor is mildly sweet and comforting. Inanchila is usually served during canaos (community feasts) and household celebrations. The dish reflects the Bontoc people's strong connection to rice culture and communal traditions.

• Bontoc Carved Wooden Artifacts (Local Product)

Bontoc is highly regarded for its traditional woodcarving, particularly shields, statues, and household tools. Local artisans carve these using hardwood and follow ancestral patterns that carry cultural meanings. Many carvings represent ancestral spirits, protection symbols, and agricultural life. Woodcarving remains a central aspect of Bontoc identity, reflecting their warrior heritage. These products are commonly sold to visitors and serve as representations of the town’s deep artistry.

5. Natonin

• Gotok (Local Food)

Gotok is a traditional soup that originated in Natonin and is cherished for its simplicity and rich flavor. It is made from pork meat or bones simmered for long hours with local herbs and vegetables. The soup is typically served hot, especially during cold seasons or after long work in the fields. Gotok reflects the practical and resourceful cooking style of Natonin people. Its comforting taste makes it a household favorite.

• Natonin Bamboo Crafts (Local Product)

Natonin produces excellent bamboo craft products such as baskets, trays, and household containers. Bamboo is abundant in the area, making it a sustainable material for traditional craftsmanship. Locals carefully cut, dry, and weave bamboo strips into strong and elegant forms. Many designs follow patterns handed down by elders, ensuring cultural continuity. These bamboo products are used daily and sold in nearby markets.

6. Sabangan

• Sabangan Etag (Local Food)

Sabangan is known for its unique style of preparing Etag, a traditionally preserved pork deeply rooted in local culture. The meat is salted and smoked for several days, absorbing the natural aroma of mountain wood. Sabangan’s version is recognized for being less salty and more aromatic due to its specific smoking method. It is commonly used to flavor soups and vegetables, especially during community feasts. This food reflects the community’s skill in traditional preservation techniques passed down through generations.

• Sabangan Loom-Woven Textiles (Local Product)

Sabangan is home to local weavers who produce loom-woven textiles featuring intricate Cordilleran patterns. These textiles are usually crafted using backstrap looms, a traditional equipment used for centuries. Each woven piece carries symbolic patterns representing local identity and ancestral heritage. The weaving process requires great patience, skill, and creativity from artisans. Sabangan woven products are often purchased as souvenirs and used during cultural celebrations.

7. Sadanga

• Kiniing (Local Food)

Kiniing is a smoked meat delicacy originating from Sadanga, where it is widely served during rituals and feasts. The meat is lightly salted, then hung over the hearth where it slowly absorbs smoke for several days. Sadanga’s kinnuday has a distinct smoky aroma that sets it apart from other versions in Mountain Province. It is commonly cooked with vegetables to add depth and flavor to local dishes. This food represents the community’s traditional method of meat preservation and communal cooking.

• Sadanga Handwoven Backpacks (Local Product)

Sadanga is known for traditional handwoven backpacks made from rattan and local vines. These backpacks, often called “kayabang,” are used for carrying vegetables, grains, and goods across mountainous trails. Each piece is carefully crafted to be durable, lightweight, and functional for everyday use. The weaving reflects the skill and resourcefulness of Sadanga artisans who continue this ancestral craft. These backpacks are not only practical but also serve as cultural symbols of the community.

8. Sagada

• Sagada Lemon Pie (Local Food)

Sagada Lemon Pie is one of the most famous delicacies originating from Sagada. It is made using native lemons that grow in the cool highland climate, giving the pie a refreshing citrus flavor. The crust is traditionally handmade and baked to achieve a perfect balance of softness and crispness. Locals and visitors enjoy this pie for its unique taste not found anywhere else in the Philippines. It has become an iconic food representing Sagada’s creativity and culinary craftsmanship.

• Sagada Arabica Coffee (Local Product)

Sagada is globally recognized for its premium Arabica coffee, grown in high-altitude plantations. The cool climate and fertile soil contribute to its smooth, rich, and slightly fruity flavor profile. Farmers handpick and sun-dry the beans using traditional methods that ensure quality. The coffee industry supports many local livelihoods and promotes sustainable agricultural practices. Sagada coffee is considered one of the finest in the Philippines and is highly sought after by coffee enthusiasts.

9. Tadian

• Inakob (Local Food)

Inakob in the municipality of Tadian, Mountain Province, is a traditional rice-wine or rice-beer brewed by the local Kankanaey community as a symbol of culture, unity, and celebration. Made from fermented rice and natural yeast, Inakob is usually prepared during important gatherings such as weddings, community feasts, harvest rituals, and family occasions. Beyond being a drink, it represents the people’s strong connection to their land and agricultural practices, as the entire brewing process—from rice cultivation to fermentation—is done with care and shared knowledge passed down through generations. Inakob remains an important cultural heritage of Tadian, reminding both locals and visitors of the community’s deep respect for tradition and the value they place on togetherness.

• Tadian Pottery (Local Product)

Tadian is known for its centuries-old tradition of pottery making, which continues to be practiced by local artisans. The clay used for pottery is sourced from local soils, giving each piece a distinctive texture and color. Artisans carefully mold, carve, and fire the clay to create jars, pots, and decorative items. Pottery plays an important role in Tadian’s culture, having been used historically for cooking and storage. Today, these handcrafted items are also treasured as cultural souvenirs representing the artistic heritage of the municipality.

10. Paracelis

• Paracelis Tableya de Cacao (Local Product)

Paracelis Tableya de Cacao is a traditional Filipino chocolate tablets (tablea) made from pure, roasted, ground, and molded cacao beans, likely sourced from or associated with Paracelis, Mountain Province in the Philippines, known for its local produce and burgeoning cacao sector, offering an authentic, rich, and slightly bitter chocolate base for drinks like tsokolate.

• Paracelis Cassava Delicacies (Local Food)

Paracelis is known for its variety of cassava-based delicacies, such as cassava cake and steamed cassava rolls. Cassava is one of the main crops of the municipality, making it a staple ingredient in local households. These delicacies are enjoyed during community feasts, gatherings, and celebrations. The recipes are passed down through generations, preserving the culinary traditions of the community.
