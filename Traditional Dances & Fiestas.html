Traditional Dances & Fiestas of Mountain Province

1. Bontoc

Pattong Dance

The Pattong is the most iconic gong dance of the Bontoc people. Men beat large flat gongs (gangsa) while women join in rhythmic movements. It represents unity and community celebration, performed during weddings, victory feasts, rites of passage, and major gatherings.

Chono / Tayaw

A graceful thanksgiving dance symbolizing peace and harmony among clans. Dancers move in a circular motion as a sign of unity and collective strength of the ili (village).

Am-among (Peace Pact Celebration)

A ritual feast celebrating the renewal of bodong or peace pacts between tribes. Traditional chants, gong beats, and ceremonial dances are performed to honor friendship and inter-community peace.

Fiesta: Lang-Ay Festival

Held every April, the Lang-Ay Festival showcases all cultural traditions of Mountain Province. It includes street dancing, cultural presentations, gong parades, and indigenous rituals.

2. Bauko

Begnas di Bauko

A sacred agricultural ritual offering thanksgiving to Kabunian for good harvest. Elders perform prayers while the community dances around the dap-ay to call for protection and prosperity.

Takyay / Tayaw

A joyful dance performed during weddings, community gatherings, and feasts. It highlights unity among families and is usually accompanied by gong music.

3. Sagada

Begnas Festival (Sagada)

A ritual that marks the start or end of the agricultural cycle. The community offers prayers, pig sacrifices, and dances around the sacred dap-ay to seek blessings.

Etag Festival

Celebrates Sagada’s signature smoked meat delicacy called etag. It features street dancing, cooking demonstrations, and cultural storytelling.

4. Besao

Tayaw di Besao

a traditional community dance performed by the Kankanaey people to celebrate important events such as weddings, thanksgiving rituals, victory feasts, community gatherings, and other major occasions. In Besao, the Tayaw is more than just a performance — it is a symbol of unity, cultural identity, and joy. The dance is typically led by respected community members called mumbaki or elders, who move gracefully in a circular or forward backward pattern while following the rhythm of gongs (gangsa). Participants join in naturally, forming lines or circles that represent equality, harmony, and togetherness within the community.

Kachangyan Dance

the Kachangyan (sometimes spelled kachagyan or kachang-yan) is a traditional Kankanaey victory and celebration dance performed during important community events such as weddings, thanksgiving feasts (begnas), canaos, and other major gatherings. Unlike the Ifugao “Kachangyan,” which is mainly a wedding dance, the Besao version is rooted in Kankanaey culture, symbolizing joy, unity, and the public honoring of esteemed individuals or families. The dance is usually led by respected elders or community members, who step gracefully in a proud, steady rhythm while following the beat of the gangsa (flat gongs). The movement is simple yet dignified—slow stepping forward and backward, swaying gently, and maintaining a poised and respectful posture. As the gongs continue, more people join the circle or line, showing community solidarity and shared happiness for the occasion.

5. Natonin

Ayog Dance

The Ayog Dance is known as eagle dance, Sapor or Torayan in other municipalities, it is a vibrant cultural dance by the Balangao and Baliwon people (Barlig, Natonin, Paracelis), mimicking eagle movements with swaying arms and bent knees, accompanied by distinct gongs (takkonak, solengfiat, sapor) for blessings and rituals, celebrating community and connection to nature, especially the eagle as a sacred messenger.

Sas Alliwa Festival

is a vibrant cultural celebration meaning "gathering of relatives & friends," featuring street dances, indigenous food, cultural programs, and games, showcasing local traditions and unity, with a recent proclamation marking its first official celebration in April 2025. It's a time for locals and visitors to experience authentic Cordilleran culture through performances, food fairs, and community events.

6. Barlig

Finayang Dance

The Eagle Dance or Finayang in Barlig, Mountain Province, Philippines, is a significant cultural performance, part of the broader indigenous tradition in the Cordilleras, often associated with honoring the eagle as a sacred messenger, involving specific gong rhythms like takkonak, solengfiat, and sapor, and showcasing distinct movements representing the bird's flight, common in gatherings and celebrations in the region.

Barlig Rammers Festival

The Barlig Rammers Festival is an annual cultural celebration held in the municipality of Barlig, honoring the unity, strength, and rich heritage of its diverse villages—Lias, Kadaclan, and Barlig Central. The word “Rammers” symbolizes the people’s determination, resilience, and hard work in overcoming challenges, much like the rugged terrain and steep rice terraces that define the landscape of Barlig. During the festival, communities gather to showcase traditional dances, indigenous games, weaving, music, and rituals that highlight the unique identities of each barangay. Street dances, parades, and cultural presentations bring to life the stories, myths, and everyday traditions of the Barlig people, especially their close relationship with the land and mountains surrounding them, including the iconic Mount Amuyao. More than just a celebration, the Barlig Rammers Festival reinforces bonds among residents, promotes local pride, and preserves the cultural practices passed down through generations—making it one of Mountain Province’s most vibrant cultural events.

7. Tadian

Lambak di ayyoweng ed Tadian

Lambak ed Tadian refers to the annual cultural festival of Ayyoweng di Lambak ed Tadian (or simply Ayyoweng Festival) in Tadian, Mountain Province, Philippines, celebrating its rich Cordilleran culture, traditions, and bountiful harvest, with the 2025 edition marking its 17th year and featuring events like road safety symposia and cultural presentations, highlighting community spirit and heritage.

8. Sabangan

Kanyaw di Sabangan

Kanyaw is more than just a feast; it’s a lively celebration of community, identity, and cultural heritage among the Igorot people of the Cordillera region. Rooted in centuries of tradition, Kanyaw represents unity, gratitude, and respect for ancestors. It brings together families and communities to honor life’s milestones through traditional dances, food, and storytelling. This tradition evolves over time while maintaining its essential values. Kanyaw strengthens social connections through communal participation, where everyone works together in preparation, cooking, and celebrating. The communal gathering of eating known as watwat and the sharing of tapey (traditional rice wine) highlight the importance of collaboration and the strength of the Igorot spirit.

Sabangan Founding Anniversary

Sabangan, Mountain Province, celebrates its Founding Anniversary on June 25th, with the year marking the specific anniversary (e.g., 2025 marked the 62nd, as seen in posts from June 2025). This significant day is declared a special non-working holiday for the municipality to allow residents to participate in celebrations, often tied into larger provincial festivals like Lang-Ay Festival.

9. Sadanga

Oggood Dance

The Say-am di Sadanga is a traditional celebration and community feast practiced in the municipality of Sadanga, deeply rooted in the cultural beliefs and rituals of the indigenous people. Held during special occasions such as weddings, thanksgiving rites, prosperity celebrations, or the honoring of respected families, the Say-am serves as a powerful symbol of unity, gratitude, and cultural continuity. During the celebration, elders perform ritual offerings and prayers to seek blessings, protection, good health, and abundance for the family or community hosting the feast. The event is accompanied by traditional dancing to the rhythm of gangsa, sharing of food, and active participation of relatives, neighbors, and village members—reflecting the strong value of innabuyog (community cooperation). More than just a festive gathering, the Say-am di Sadanga is a living expression of identity and tradition, reminding the younger generation of their deep connection to their ancestors, their land, and the customs that continue to shape community life.

Ga’dang Festival

The Ga'dang Festival isn't a singular, widely-known event like Sinulog, but rather refers to cultural expressions of the Ga'dang people, an indigenous group in Paracelis, Mountain Province, Philippines, celebrating their unique identity, language ("high ground"), and traditions through dance (like the "Kopyan chi Biyeg" performed by Parangal Dance Company) that honor gods, spirits, and bountiful harvests. It's a rich display of Cordilleran heritage, focusing on thanksgiving, cultural rituals, and community unity, often featuring vibrant traditional attire and performances.
