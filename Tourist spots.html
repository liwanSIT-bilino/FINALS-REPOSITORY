Major Tourist Spots in Mountain Province

Barlig

Mt. Amuyao – Mount Amuyao is one of the highest and most important mountains in the Cordillera region. It rises 2,702 meters above sea level and is located between Barlig in Mountain Province and Banaue in Ifugao. Because of its height, it has a very cold climate, thick fog, and forests covered in moss. What makes Mount Amuyao special is its unique environment and cultural surroundings. The mountain is home to mossy forests, pine trees, and a rich variety of plants that only grow in high places. The air is fresh, and the views from the summit include wide landscapes, mountain ranges, and sometimes a sea of clouds, which many hikers love to witness during sunrise.

Lawi – "LAWI" in Barlig, Mountain Province, likely refers to a local landmark or cultural element, possibly the Philippine Eagle Statue, symbolizing conservation and culture, or maybe a localized term (like a clan, spot, or tradition) within one of Barlig's 11 barangays, a municipality known for its Cordilleran culture and scenic beauty in the Cordillera Administrative Region of the Philippines.

Barlig Rice Terraces – The Barlig Rice Terraces are one of the cultural and agricultural treasures of Mountain Province. Found in the municipality of Barlig, these terraces were carved by local ancestors into the mountain slopes using traditional tools and indigenous knowledge passed down through generations. These terraces show how the Barlig people adapted to the steep terrain of the Cordillera. By cutting the mountains into step-like layers, they created flat fields where they could grow rice despite the rugged landscape. The terraces also help manage water by allowing it to flow gently from one level to another, which is important for maintaining healthy rice fields. Barlig’s rice terraces are known for their mossy forests surrounding them, their cool mountain climate, and the peaceful, less-commercialized environment compared to more famous terraces in other provinces. Because Barlig is home to indigenous communities like Kadaclan and Fang-od, the terraces also reflect the people’s identity, traditions, and deep connection to their land.

Bauko

Mount Data National Park – Mount Data National Park is one of the most important protected areas in the Cordillera region. Located between Bauko, Mountain Province and Atok, Benguet Atok, it covers a wide area of pine forests, mossy forests, rivers, and mountain ecosystems. The park sits at a very high elevation—around 2,310 meters above sea level—which gives it a cold climate similar to the highlands of Baguio but even more pristine and untouched.

Payangapang pool – Pangayapang Pool is a natural cold pool located in Barlig, Mountain Province, known for its refreshing waters and peaceful environment. It is fed by clean mountain springs, making the pool naturally cold throughout the year. Because it is surrounded by pine trees, rice terraces, and mountain scenery, the place feels quiet and relaxing—perfect for visitors who want to cool off or enjoy nature.

Besao

Tagpeo Falls – Tagpeo Falls is one of the hidden natural gems of Mountain Province, known for its refreshing waters, quiet surroundings, and untouched beauty. Tucked away in the highland forests and steep terrains typical of the Cordillera, the falls attract nature lovers, hikers, and local residents who enjoy serene, less-commercialized destinations. The trek to Tagpeo Falls often involves passing through traditional footpaths, rice terraces, and mountain slopes, giving visitors a glimpse of the local landscape and community life. Its cool waters flow into a natural pool ideal for relaxation, while the surrounding vegetation creates a peaceful, refreshing atmosphere. Tagpeo Falls is treasured not only for its scenic charm but also for the sense of calm and connection to nature it provides — a perfect escape for those seeking quiet moments away from town centers.

Bontoc

Maligcong Rice Terraces – The Maligcong Rice Terraces are among the most beautiful and photogenic terraces in Mountain Province. Located in Brgy. Maligcong, Bontoc, these terraces are known for their wide, bowl-shaped structure that stretches across the mountains, creating a dramatic landscape. What makes Maligcong special is its untouched, peaceful environment. Unlike other popular terraces, Maligcong is less commercialized, so visitors can enjoy quiet scenery, fresh air, and an authentic Cordilleran atmosphere. The terraces are maintained by the local community using traditional farming techniques passed down through generations. During sunrise, the terraces are often covered with a sea of clouds, which makes the area one of the top photography and viewing spots in the province.

Mt. Kupapey – Mt. Kupapey (also spelled Kupapey/Kopapey) is the most famous mountain overlooking the Maligcong Rice Terraces. It is the best sunrise viewpoint in the area, offering a panoramic view of the terraces surrounded by mountains and, on good days, a thick sea of clouds. The hike to Mt. Kupapey is moderate and usually takes 45 minutes to 1.5 hours, depending on the hiker’s pace. Along the trail, you’ll pass through pine forests and scenic ridges. The summit is spacious and grassy, perfect for watching the sun rise over one of the most stunning rice terraces in the Cordillera. Because of its accessibility and peaceful environment, Kupapey has become a favorite destination of hikers, photographers, and nature lovers.

Bontoc Museum – The Bontoc Museum, located in Bontoc town proper, is one of the most important cultural museums in the Cordillera. It was founded by the Sisters of the Immaculate Heart of Mary, with the goal of preserving the history, artifacts, and traditions of the Bontoc people and other indigenous groups of the Mountain Province. Inside the museum, visitors can see: Traditional weapons, tools, and household items Indigenous clothing, heirlooms, and weaving artifacts Old photographs showing the life of early Bontoc communities Miniature displays of traditional ili (villages) and stone houses Outside the main building is a reconstructed Bontoc Village, featuring actual traditional huts such as the ato, ifah, and fayu, allowing visitors to visualize how early Bontoc communities lived. The museum plays a vital role in cultural preservation by helping both locals and tourists understand the identity, values, and history of the Cordillera people.

Natonin

The Elephant head – One of the most unique natural attractions in eastern Mountain Province is the Elephant Head, a striking rock formation located along the rugged mountainsides of Natonin. As its name suggests, the formation resembles the shape of a giant elephant’s head when viewed from the road—complete with a clearly defined “trunk” and “forehead” carved naturally by time, wind, and rain. Travelers heading through the municipality often stop by the roadside to admire and photograph this distinct landmark, which has become one of Natonin’s most recognizable features.

Chuppen ay falls – Chuppen-ay Falls is one of the lesser-known natural attractions tucked within the mountains of Mountain Province, admired for its calm atmosphere, cool waters, and untouched natural setting. Surrounded by dense vegetation, traditional footpaths, and terraced landscapes typical of the Cordillera region, the falls offer a refreshing escape for hikers, locals, and visitors looking for a peaceful spot away from busier tourist destinations. The water cascades gently into a natural pool, where visitors can relax, take a dip, or simply enjoy the soothing sound of flowing water.

Paracelis

Buskalan Border Trails – The term "Buscalan Border Trail" is not an official, recognized trail name; rather, it refers to the general route used to reach Buscalan village from the Mountain Province side, which crosses the provincial border into Kalinga. The road leading from the Mountain Province to the Buscalan jump-off point in Kalinga is often subject to closures.

Banano Sea of clouds – The river systems of Paracelis are among the town’s most refreshing natural attractions, known for their wide, flowing waters and scenic surroundings that contrast beautifully with the lowland valleys and rolling hills of the eastern Cordillera. These rivers—particularly the well-known Chalalo River and its tributaries—serve as lifelines for local communities, supporting irrigation, daily activities, and small-scale farming in one of the province’s warmest and more agriculture-driven municipalities. Their clear, cool currents carve through rocky banks and forested slopes, creating natural swimming spots and peaceful areas where residents and visitors enjoy picnics, relaxation, and quiet moments by the water. The river landscapes also showcase Paracelis’ diverse terrain, where mountain ridges gradually open into fertile plains. While not yet developed as major tourist spots, the rivers of Paracelis continue to draw interest for their natural charm, accessibility, and the serene rural atmosphere they provide—offering a refreshing escape for anyone exploring the eastern side of Mountain Province.

Sabangan

Mt. Kabunian – Mt. Kabunian in Sabangan is a culturally significant and scenic mountain located in the municipality of Sabangan, Mountain Province. Like many mountains in the Cordillera, it is named after Kabunian, the supreme deity in local indigenous belief systems. Because of this, the area is considered sacred and respected by the community. Unlike the rugged limestone peaks of the Kabunian in Benguet, the Sabangan Mt. Kabunian is characterized by: Forested slopes Pine-covered ridges Nearby rice terraces Quiet village surroundings The mountain forms part of the dramatic landscape that surrounds Sabangan, which lies along the Halsema Highway between Bauko and Bontoc.

Mount Kalawitan – Mount Kalawitan is a challenging, high-altitude peak in the Cordilleras (Mountain Province, Philippines), known as one of the country's highest (around 2,714-2,922 MASL), featuring stunning mossy forests, pine trees, dwarf bamboos, breathtaking sea of clouds, and offering an advanced, multi-day hiking experience with rich indigenous culture.

Sadanga

Fuwa-as Falls – Fuwas Falls is one of the quiet and refreshing natural attractions found in the highland landscapes of Mountain Province, known for its cool waters, lush surroundings, and serene atmosphere. Hidden among forested slopes and traditional footpaths, the falls offer visitors a peaceful escape away from town centers and busy roads. Water cascades gracefully down rock formations into a natural pool, creating a soothing environment ideal for swimming, picnicking, or simply enjoying the calming sound of the rushing water. The trek to Fuwas Falls often passes through rice terraces and mountain trails, giving visitors a glimpse of local farming life and the untouched beauty of the Cordillera. Though not heavily commercialized, Fuwas Falls is cherished by locals and travelers who appreciate raw nature, making it a perfect spot for those seeking quiet adventure and a deeper connection with the natural landscapes of Mountain Province.

Belwang rice terraces – Betwagan Rice Terraces and the Nearby Villages | riderakoThe Belwang Rice Terraces refer to a lesser-known, hidden gem area near Batad in Ifugao, Philippines, offering spectacular, less-crowded trekking adventures through impressive, ancient rice paddies carved into the mountains by the Ifugao people thousands of years ago, known for their rugged beauty and remoteness compared to the more famous Banaue terraces.

Angoten and Kansel Caves – The Angoten and Kansel Caves of Sadanga are among the municipality’s most intriguing natural and cultural treasures, offering visitors a glimpse into both the geological beauty and ancestral traditions of the community. Tucked within the steep limestone cliffs and forested slopes of Sadanga, these caves are known for their dramatic rock formations, cool interiors, and quiet, untouched atmosphere. Their natural chambers, stalactites, and rugged pathways reflect the ancient landscape that has shaped the lives of the people for generations.

Sagada

Hanging Coffins – One of the most iconic heritage sites in the Philippines.The people of Sagada follow a unique burial ritual. The elderly carve their own coffins out of hollowed logs. If they are too weak or ill, their families prepare their coffins instead. The dead are placed inside their coffins (sometimes breaking their bones in the process of fitting them in), and the coffins are brought to a cave for burial. This is just one of the many ancient rituals and traditions that the people of the Cordillera Administrative Region (collectively known as Igorots) of Northern Philippines have preserved.

Sumaguing Cave – The Sumaguing Cave, located in the iconic tourist town of Sagada, is one of the most breathtaking natural wonders in the Cordillera region and the largest cave chamber open to visitors in the area. Often called the “Big Cave,” Sumaguing is famous for its immense underground chambers filled with dramatic limestone formations, crystal-clear pools, and centuries-old rock shapes naturally sculpted by water. Inside, visitors encounter stunning formations that resemble curtains, kings’ thrones, giant turtles, and flowing cascades—each one shaped slowly over thousands of years.

Bomod-ok Falls – Bomod-ok Falls, also known as the “Big Falls,” is one of the most iconic natural attractions in Sagada. Towering at approximately 200 feet, it is famous for its powerful cascade that drops into a wide, refreshing natural pool surrounded by cliffs, lush vegetation, and rice terraces. Its impressive height and strong flow make it one of the grandest and most photographed waterfalls in the entire Cordillera region.

Tadian

Bessang Pass – Although the historic Bessang Pass—site of the famous World War II Battle of Bessang Pass—lies in the province of Ilocos Sur, the municipality of Tadian serves as one of the closest and most scenic gateways to this landmark. From several viewpoints in Tadian, travelers can enjoy sweeping views of the rugged mountain ranges that form the natural border between the Cordillera highlands and the Ilocos region. The area is known for its cool climate, pine-covered ridges, winding roads, and stunning highland vistas that lead toward the Bessang Pass Natural Monument. Many visitors exploring western Mountain Province choose Tadian as a starting point for learning about the historical significance of Bessang Pass, where Filipino guerrillas and Allied forces fought a decisive battle that contributed to the liberation of Northern Luzon during WWII. Today, the route toward Bessang Pass from the Tadian side offers not just historical value but also a picturesque drive through some of the most striking landscapes of the Cordillera..

Kayan Rice Terraces – The Kayan Rice Terraces of Tadian, Mountain Province, stand as one of the community’s greatest cultural and agricultural treasures. Carved by the skilled hands of the Kankana-ey people, these terraces showcase a harmonious blend of human ingenuity and the natural contours of the land. Much like the more famous terraces of Ifugao, the Kayan Rice Terraces reflect centuries-old knowledge passed down through generations—knowledge of hydrology, soil management, and communal labor systems such as innabuyog (bayanihan spirit). The terraces turn vibrant green during the planting season and golden during harvest, offering travelers a breathtaking view of layered fields stretching across the mountainsides.

Lake Gawaan – Lake Gawaan is one of the hidden natural gems of the Cordillera, nestled deep in the quiet highlands of Besao. Surrounded by pine forests, mossy grounds, and peaceful mountain air, the lake offers a serene escape that feels almost untouched by time. Its calm waters mirror the sky and surrounding trees, creating a tranquil atmosphere perfect for slow walks, reflection, and appreciating nature in its purest form..
